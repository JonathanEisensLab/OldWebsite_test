Evolution of RecA

Some interesting features of the evolution of RecA proteins:

  • Arabidopsis thaliana encodes a nuclear gene for a RecA protein that functions in the chloroplast and is evolutionarily related to cyanobacterial RecA genes, suggesting that it was transfered to the nucleus of plants sometime after the endosymbiosis with the chloroplast evolved.
  • Many Mycobacterial species have inteins (protein introns which are removed post-translationally) in their RecA proteins (see
    Saveset al 2000 for review).
  • Myxococcus xanthus encodes two RecAs genes (see Norioka et al.1995)
  • Lactococcus lactis encodes an usual RecA-like protein on the phage resistance plasmid pNP40. This gene is next to a UmuC-like gene. The functions of these proteins are not well characterized.
  • Spiroplasma citri has a recA-like gene but it only has ~390 bp similar to other recAs. S. melliferum encodes a complete recA gene but it contains a stop codon early in the gene. Both species are highly UV sensitive.
  • The pea-aphid endosymbiont Buchnera aphidicola does not encode a recA homolog in its complete genome (Shigenobu et al, 2000)

Use of RecA as a phylogenetic marker:

  • Lloyd and Sharp (1993) first addressed the possibility of using phylogenetic analysis of recA genes as a tool to study bacterial evolutionary relationships.
  • I followed up their analysis using the sequences of 40 additional recA genes that have been entered into the databases through 1995. (Eisen, 1995). In this study I showed that RecA based phylogenies are very similar to rRNA based phylogenies from the same species and that RecA trees have similar powers of resolution. For more detail about this study see Notes on Eisen 1995
  • Tanja Gruber and I used RecA comparisons to examine the phylogeny of species in the Chlorobium and Chloroflexus genera (Gruber et al 1998) For more detail about this see Notes on Gruber et al 1998.
  • Advantages of using RecA as a phylogenetic marker for bacteria
    • Relatively easy to clone for a protein encoding gene
    • Conserved function among bacteria
    • Sequence conservation varies across molecule
    • Alignments are essentially unambiguous
    • Sequence information can be helpful in creating recA- mutants
    • 3D crystal structure of E. coli RecA available
  • Disadvantages of using RecA as a phylogenetic marker for bacteria
    • Multiple divergent genes in at least one species (M. xanthus)
    • Number of sequences still 100 fold lower than for SS-rRNA
    • Similarity to Archaeal and Eukaryotic proteins somewhat low
  • Other studies using RecA sequences for phylogenetic or evolutionary studies of bacteria
    • Kullen, et al. Phylogeneetic analysis of bifidobacteria using recA
    • Nowak, et al. 1995, Nowak , et al. 1996. Genotyping of acinetobacters using recA PCR-RFLPs.
    • Van Waasbergen et al. 1996. Abstracts of ASM 96: 404. Phylogeny of deep subsurface Arthrobacter.
    • Feil, et al. recA sequences to study recombination and genetic exchange in Neisseria.
    • Vandamme et al 2000 used recA sequences to study the population structure of Burkholderia stabilis
    • Kuipers et al 2000 used analysis of RecA diversity within H. pylori to study population structure
    • Smith et al 2000 used analysis of RecA to study population structure of Neisseria
    • Byun et al 2000 used analysis of RecA to study evolution of pathogenic Vibrios
    • Blackwood et al 2000 used analysis of RecA for the identification of Mycobacterium species
    • Marechal et al 2000 used phylogenetic analysis of RecA to study the relationships of Frankia and Acidothermus

RecA-like genes in eukaryotes and Archaea

  • Archaea: Sandler et al. (1996) reported the cloning and characteriztion of RecA-like genes from a variety of Archaea.
  • Eukaryotes: Stassen et al. (1997) studied the evolution of RecA-like proteins (Rad51, DMC1, etc) in eukaryotes.
